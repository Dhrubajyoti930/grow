It was 1780. Luigi Galvani sat in his laboratory, the air thick with the scent of metallic salts and damp stone. Beside him lay a frog, dissected, its legs exposed to the cold curiosity of science.

He touched his metal scalpel to the nerve, and then—an impossible movement. The dead limbs jerked. They convulsed as if a ghost had reached from the ether to command the muscle.

Galvani had stumbled upon animal electricity. He believed the spark lived within the biology itself, a vital force waiting for a conduit. Though he was half-wrong—it was the contact of two different metals that created the current—he had sparked the dawn of bioelectromagnetics.

Today, every heartbeat and every thought is a continuation of that twitching rhythm.
